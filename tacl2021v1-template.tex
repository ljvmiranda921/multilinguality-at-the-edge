\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have made remarkable progress in recent years and are increasingly integrated into everyday work and life \citep{microsoft2026aidiffusion}.
This progress is evident in both multilingual coverage, with models now speaking hundreds of languages \citep{ustun-etal-2024-aya,gemmateam2025gemma3technicalreport},
and in efficiency, with increasingly capable edge models running on commodity hardware \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{treviso-etal-2023-efficient}.
Yet despite these advances, a wide gap in adoption persists between the Global North and the Global South \citep{khan2024ailics,pava2025mind}.
We can point several factors that contribute to this disparity.
Most LLMs are not trained on the long tail of the world's 7,000 languages and thus lack the performance necessary for downstream tasks \citep{occhini2026artificialintelligencecreatingnew}.
In addition, infrastructure constraints such as limited connectivity and low smartphone penetration, especially in the Global South, further restrict the use of both cloud-based and on-device models \citep{GSMA2025MobileEconomy,itu-2025-facts}.
As LLMs become central to economic productivity, these compounding barriers threaten to widen the divide further.

Tackling the problems of efficiency or multilinguality in isolation has merit, but it risks not addressing the actual needs of the communities left behind this widening gap.
For example, research on model compression has largely focused on English, with several studies observing degradation in multilingual performance as models are made smaller \citep{ogueji-etal-2022-intriguing,mohammadshahi-etal-2022-compressed,marchisio-etal-2024-quantization}.
Similarly, multilingual scaling laws \citep{longpre2025atlasadaptivetransferscaling,he-etal-2025-scaling} reveal a power-law relationship between multilingual performance and model scale, suggesting that smaller, more deployable models may struggle with low-resource languages.
Approaching these lines of work in tandem is important, as \citet{ahia-etal-2021-low-resource} argue that language communities face not only data constraints but also compute limitations (the ``low-resource double bind'').
This view is echoed by \citet{nigatu-etal-2024-zenos}, who show that ``low-resource'' as a term pertains to technology as much as to data.
Building on these insights, we adopt this framing as a foundational premise and refer to the intersection of multilinguality and edge-efficiency as the \textit{last mile} of NLP.
We use this term because it captures the hardest and most costly stretch of LLM development: reaching communities that need both a model that understands their language and the efficiency to run on their available hardware.

In this work, we survey \numpapers{} methods, techniques, and challenges within the \textit{last mile} of NLP.
We first motivate why studying multilinguality at the edge is relevant (\S\ref{sec:motivation}), highlighting a clear need, a strong research opportunity, and a growing urgency.
We organize our discussion around the large language modeling pipeline (\S\ref{sec:lm_pipeline}), covering data collection, development, and deployment, and describe relevant techniques at each stage.
We then define the constraints of efficient NLP (\S\ref{sec:edge_constraints})
and the requirements for building quality multilingual models (\S\ref{sec:multilingual_constraints}),
and highlight how the two often impose competing demands (\S\ref{sec:the_tension}),
which is precisely what makes the \textit{last mile} so challenging.
Our survey (\S\ref{sec:survey}) then examines how these tensions manifest and are being addressed at each stage of the LLM pipeline (see Figure~\ref{fig:lm_pipeline}).

\subsection{The Language Modelling Pipeline}
\label{sec:lm_pipeline}

We structure the pipeline based on design choices highlighted across recent open LLM model releases such as the
OLMo \citep{walsh2025,olmo2025olmo3},
Nemotron \citep{nvidia2025nvidianemotronnano2,nvidia2025nvidianemotron3efficient}, and Aya \citep{ustun-etal-2024-aya,dang2024ayaexpansecombiningresearch,salamanca2026tinyayabridgingscale} model series.
In general, we describe five main stages:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=5mm,topsep=0mm,itemsep=0mm]
  \item \textbf{Data Collection}: sourcing and curating the text corpora used for both pretraining and post-training.
        Pretraining corpora tend to be unstructured web text, while post-training datasets are more structured, such as prompt-response or preference pairs.
  \item \textbf{Pretraining}: training the base model on large-scale corpora to learn general language representations.
        This is typically the most computationally expensive stage; for example, \citet{nvidia2025nvidianemotronnano2} pretrain on 20T tokens, while \citet{olmo2025olmo3} use 8$\times$ NVIDIA H100s for around 6T tokens.
  \item \textbf{Post-training}: further adapting and tuning the base model toward specific capabilities through techniques such as supervised finetuning \citep[SFT,][]{ouyang2022traininglanguagemodelsfollow} and reinforcement learning.
        We also group model compression and merging under this stage.
  \item \textbf{Inference}: serving the model on a target device, which can range from microcontrollers to data center-scale GPUs in terms of storage and compute capabilities.
  \item \textbf{Evaluation}: measuring model performance across a variety of capabilities and domains. This involves assessing performance on static benchmarks \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{singh-etal-2025-global,adelani-etal-2022-masakhaner} or on structured testbeds \citep{reddi-etal-2020-mlperf,peng2023efficiencypentathlonstandardizedarena}.
\end{itemize}

The design choices made at each stage jointly determine how well a model serves multilingual communities on constrained hardware.
