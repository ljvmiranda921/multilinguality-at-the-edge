\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have made remarkable progress in recent years and are increasingly integrated into everyday work and life \citep{microsoft2026aidiffusion}.
This progress is evident in both multilingual coverage, with models now speaking hundreds of languages \citep{ustun-etal-2024-aya,gemmateam2025gemma3technicalreport},
and in efficiency, with increasingly capable edge models running on commodity hardware \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{treviso-etal-2023-efficient}.
Yet despite these advances, a wide gap in adoption persists between the Global North and the Global South \citep{khan2024ailics,pava2025mind}.
We can point several factors that contribute to this disparity.
Most LLMs are not trained on the long tail of the world's 7,000 languages and thus lack the performance necessary for downstream tasks \citep{occhini2026artificialintelligencecreatingnew}.
In addition, infrastructure constraints such as limited connectivity and low smartphone penetration, especially in the Global South, further restrict the use of both cloud-based and on-device models \citep{GSMA2025MobileEconomy,itu-2025-facts}.
As LLMs become central to economic productivity, these compounding barriers threaten to widen the divide further.

Tackling the problems of efficiency or multilinguality in isolation has merit, but it risks not addressing the actual needs of the communities left behind this widening gap.
For example, research on model compression has largely focused on English, with several studies observing degradation in multilingual performance as models are made smaller \citep{ogueji-etal-2022-intriguing,mohammadshahi-etal-2022-compressed,marchisio-etal-2024-quantization}.
Similarly, multilingual scaling laws \citep{longpre2025atlasadaptivetransferscaling,he-etal-2025-scaling} reveal a power-law relationship between multilingual performance and model scale, suggesting that smaller, more deployable models may struggle with low-resource languages.
Approaching these lines of work in tandem is important, as \citet{ahia-etal-2021-low-resource} argue that language communities face not only data constraints but also compute limitations (the ``low-resource double bind'').
This view is echoed by \citet{nigatu-etal-2024-zenos}, who show that ``low-resource'' as a term pertains to technology as much as to data.
Building on these insights, we then ask: \textit{``how can we develop language technologies and deploy them nearest to the communities that need them the most?''}
We refer to this challenge as the \textit{last mile}, which refers to the intersection of multilinguality and edge deployment, reaching communities that need both a model that understands their language and the efficiency to run on their available hardware devices.
This challenge is non-trivial, as we show that sometimes, prioritizing edge efficiency can come at the cost of capability, and vice-versa.

% The \textit{last mile} refers to the hardest and most costly stretch of Language AI development, i.e., developing and deploying language technologies nearest to the communities that need them the most%.: reaching communities that need both a model that understands their language and the efficiency to run on their available hardware.

In this work, we define the challenge of the \textit{last mile} (\S\ref{sec:motivation}) and present a survey of \numpapers{} papers that describe methods attempting to solve this problem at different stages of the language modelling pipeline (\S\ref{sec:lm_pipeline}).
For each stage of the pipeline, we describe how constraints present in deploying edge models (\ref{sec:edge_constraints}) can come into tension with the properties necessary to build capable multilingual models (\ref{sec:multilingual_constraints}).
Although we've been seeing LLM releases that jointly address the \textit{last mile} (e.g., Tiny Aya \citep{salamanca2026tinyayabridgingscale} with support for 70 languages while being under 3.35B and Omnilingual ASR \citep{omnilingualasrteam2025omnilingualasropensourcemultilingual} that covers 1.6k languages with a 300M parameter variant),
our desideratum is to stimulate more work at this intersection of multilingual and edge NLP,
and so, we discuss opportunities on how it can be done in practice and provide actionable recommendations for different stakeholders (\S\ref{sec:discussion}).
Finally, we hope that this work contributes to the development of equitable and inclusive language technologies.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

To understand the landscape of Multilingual Edge LLMs,
we collect research from 2021 onwards on multilinguality, edge and efficient NLP, and work that combines both.
In total, we obtain \numpapers{} papers.
The rest of this section describes our methodology.

\paragraph{Initial Paper Screening}
First, we gather studies from peer-reviewed academic papers, industry whitepapers, and technical reports using the Semantic Scholar API.\footnote{\url{https://www.semanticscholar.org/product/api}}
Our search query includes keywords relating to multilingual NLP, efficiency, and LLM deployment (Appendix \ref{appendix:initial_paper_screening}).
This step resulted in 2,473 papers.
We further screened for relevance by filtering for citations in a staggered manner, i.e., papers published in 2021 should have $\geq 100$ citations while those published last year (2025) should have at least 3.

\paragraph{Filtering and Annotation}
For each work, we perform human and LLM-assisted filtering and annotation to identify key attributes such as the work's
focus in the LLM pipeline,
whether it focuses on multilinguality, efficiency, or both,
and deployment device.
We bootstrap annotations with GPT 4.1 Mini\footnote{Version: \texttt{gpt-4.1-mini-2025-04-14}} by including the work's title and abstract in a prompt shown in \autoref{fig:appendix_annotation_prompt},
and then perform human validation to correct the annotations.

\paragraph{Final Validation}
Finally, we conduct a round of human validation to correct and refine the annotations.
In total, we collected \numpapers{} works for each stage of the LLM pipeline.
Appendix \ref{appendix:extended_survey_methodology} provides details of our collection methodology.

\subsection{Why Study the Two Fields Together?}
\label{sec:motivation}

While Edge LLM and Multilinguality have each been studied in isolation,
we observe trends suggesting that they must be studied together.

\paragraph{Need: Multilingual communities face infrastructure constraints}
The first trend is that communities with the highest linguistic diversity often face the most severe infrastructure constraints. %  such as network connectivity or ICT adoption.
As shown in \autoref{fig:infra_lingdiv_ict}, countries such as Papua New Guinea, Nigeria, and Chad are linguistically diverse yet among the least active on the Internet.
This compounds their disadvantage in the LLM pipeline: most pretraining data are derived from web-scale text, which these communities lack due to limited Internet activity, and
the dominant paradigm for serving LLMs relies on cloud infrastructure (i.e., requests are sent over a network).% this limited connectivity restricts  how these communities can access language technologies.
The result is LLMs that are both unable to serve their languages and undeployable in their specific contexts.
In addition, entry-level smartphones cost up to 48\% of monthly income for the poorest quintile in these regions, meaning the devices that do exist are mostly low-end \cite{GSMA2025MobileEconomy}.
Of the 21 countries with more than 100 living languages, 12 are classified as low or lower-middle income, showing a \textit{need} for models that can run entirely on-device.

\subsection{The Constraints the Edge Demands}
\label{sec:edge_constraints}

In this work, we define the \textit{Edge LLM} field as the set of methods and techniques for developing and deploying language models in constrained settings.
These settings typically involve commodity hardware such as mobile phones and laptops, and sometimes even microcontroller devices.
Edge LLM is relevant because much of the Global South is constrained in digital infrastructure \citep{GSMA2025MobileEconomy,occhini2026artificialintelligencecreatingnew}, and the usual routes for deploying LLMs, such as using high-end GPUs or cloud services, are often inaccessible.
Successful Edge LLM deployment has been seen in domains such as healthcare \citep{hu-etal-2025-nlp-public-health-africa,rutunda2026llmfrontline}, agriculture \citep{singh2024farmerchatscalingaipoweredagricultural,samuel2025agrollmconnectingfarmersagricultural}, and law \citep{ariai-etal-2025-nlp-legal-domain}.
Based on \citet{treviso-etal-2023-efficient} and \citet{zheng2025reviewedgeLLM}, we identify \textbf{constraints} for effective Edge LLM deployment.

\paragraph{Memory}
The amount of memory in a device limits what LLMs can be loaded and served.
It is also relevant during finetuning, where it constrains the size of a training batch.
For example, mobile DRAM is typically 6--12\,GB \citep{liu2024mobilellm}, yet a 7B-parameter model already requires over 8\,GB in FP16.

% For training, this can restrict the batch size available during training.
% This deployment consideration 
% Limits model size that can be deployed for inference, the batch size during training, and the KV cache during long interactions.
% \ljm{For example, \citet{zheng2025reviewedgeLLM} note that a 7B-parameter model requires over 8\,GB of memory in FP16, which already exceeds the capacity of many mobile devices and makes quantization a necessity rather than an optimization.}

\paragraph{Compute}
The processing power of edge devices governs both the feasibility of finetuning techniques and the latency of models during inference.
The gap between LLM requirements (in TFLOPs) and edge device throughput (in TOPS) continues to widen \citep{zheng2025reviewedgeLLM}.

\paragraph{Energy}
Sustained inference can quickly drain a device's battery, restricting how long and how intensively a model can run.
For example, inference energy can vary by a factor of over 1,000 across models \citep{luccioni-etal-2024-power},
and even quantized LLMs on a Raspberry Pi consume several joules per token \citep{husom-etal-2025-sustainable}.
