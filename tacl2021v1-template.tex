\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have made remarkable progress in recent years and are increasingly integrated into everyday work and life \citep{microsoft2026aidiffusion}.
This progress is evident in both multilingual coverage, with models now speaking hundreds of languages \citep{ustun-etal-2024-aya,gemmateam2025gemma3technicalreport},
and in efficiency, with increasingly capable edge models running on commodity hardware \citep{treviso-etal-2023-efficient,zheng2025reviewedgeLLM}.
Yet despite these advances, a wide gap in adoption persists between the Global North and the Global South \citep{khan2024ailics,pava2025mind}.
We can point to several factors that contribute to this disparity.
Most LLMs aren't trained on the long tail of the world's 7,000 languages and thus lack the performance necessary for downstream tasks \citep{occhini2026artificialintelligencecreatingnew}.
In addition, infrastructure constraints further limit access as, for instance in Sub-Saharan Africa, more than half of the network is in 2G-3G and 54\% are in smartphones \citep{GSMA2025MobileEconomy,itu-2025-facts}, limiting the use of both cloud-based and on-device models.
This entanglement between multilingual performance, which is often rooted in data, and compute infrastructure has been described by \citet{ahia-etal-2021-low-resource} as the ``low-resource double bind'' and by \citet{nigatu-etal-2024-zenos} as the ``Zeno's paradox in low-resource.''
Both works describe how this scarcity is multidimensional and that intervening on one scarcity over the other, i.e., focusing only on efficiency or multilinguality, risks not addressing the needs of the communities left behind this widening gap.
