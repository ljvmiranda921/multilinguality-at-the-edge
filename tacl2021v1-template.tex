\begin{abstract}
  As large language models (LLMs) become central to economic and civic life, where and how they're deployed determines who benefits from them.
  Yet LLM development has optimized toward a frontier defined by performance and efficiency alone, while
  data scarcity,
  infrastructure gaps, and
  institutional readiness,
  the constraints that most determine access, remain unaddressed.
  We introduce the Global Access Frontier, drawing on growth diagnostics to reframe deployment as a problem of access.
  Surveying \numpapers{} papers from 2021 onward, we organize interventions by the constraint they relax.
  We find that
  dominant data interventions inherit gaps from their source models,
  infrastructure work targets high-compute contexts not used in the Global South,
  and national AI strategies lag behind the systems they govern.
  We also demonstrate how to identify a community's binding constraint via a case study on clinical health workers in Rwanda.
  We hope that the Global Access Frontier will serve as both a diagnostic tool and a call to action for more inclusive language technology development.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Where and how large language models (LLMs) are deployed determines who can benefit from them.
But in practice, deployment decisions focus on just two dimensions: capability (how well the model performs) and size (how many parameters it has).
The field has progressed along these dimensions:
scaling laws have shown that model capabilities improve predictably with increased parameters, data, and compute \citep{kaplan2020scalinglawsneurallanguage,wei2022emergent},
while distillation and compression techniques have enabled deploying smaller LLMs on mobile and embedded devices \citep{schwartz-etal-2020-green,treviso-etal-2023-efficient}.
Research progress has thus coalesced around the \textit{Efficiency Frontier}: achieving the best possible performance at a given model size \citep{rae2022scalinglanguagemodelsmethods,hoffmann2022trainingcomputeoptimallargelanguage}.
In most frontier LLM releases, this is typically visualized with cost on the x-axis (parameters, FLOPs, latency) and benchmark performance on the y-axis \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{liu2024mobilellm,allal2025smollm,walsh2025}.

However, optimizing the Efficiency Frontier leaves critical constraints unaddressed.
The frontier assumes that the \textit{deployment context}, the environment into which a model is deployed, varies only in compute budget,
but real-world deployment is constrained along multiple dimensions simultaneously.
For example, LLMs support only a fraction of the world's 7,000 languages due to limited training data, and multilingual benchmarks reveal that LLM performance degrades sharply for low-resource languages \citep{singh-etal-2024-aya,ustun-etal-2024-aya}.
In addition, adoption in the Global South lags at half the rate of the Global North due to limited infrastructure caused by network connectivity or expensive hardware \citep{khan2024ailics,misra2025measuringaidiffusionpopulationnormalized,microsoft2026aidiffusion}.
As LLMs become central to economic productivity, entire language communities risk exclusion not because efficient models do not exist, but because the constraints they face lie outside the Efficiency Frontier's framework.


%\paragraph{Global Access Frontier}
In this work, we introduce the \textbf{Global Access Frontier} (\autoref{fig:global_access_frontier}) and reframe language model deployment as a problem of access (\S\ref{sec:global_access_frontier}).
This framing draws on growth diagnostics by \citet{hausmann2005growth}, which argues that development interventions fail when they target the wrong constraint: progress requires identifying and relaxing the \textit{binding} one.
A constraint is binding if it is a bottleneck, i.e., relaxing it improves outcomes, while relaxing others does not.
The same logic applies here:
a compression technique like quantization can improve efficiency yet disproportionately degrade performance on non-Latin scripts \citep{marchisio-etal-2024-quantization};
a multilingual LLM can score well on standard benchmarks yet fail on evaluations designed by language communities \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{wilie-etal-2020-indonlu,adelani-etal-2025-irokobench,ojo-etal-2025-afrobench,miranda-etal-2025-filbench}.
We argue that efficiency alone, as exemplified by the Efficiency Frontier, does not ensure access.

Using this framework, we contextualize existing literature (\S\ref{sec:survey_of_interventions}) from 2021 onward, organizing interventions by the constraint they relax:
training and evaluation data,
hardware and network infrastructure,
and language community readiness (\autoref{fig:taxonomy}).
For each constraint, we discuss relevant literature and a broad analysis.
In this study, we find that
(1)
\textbf{interventions are not a panacea}, even appealing approaches such as synthetic data generation can introduce biases and higher costs due to most LLMs not pretrained on low-resource languages (\S\ref{sec:data_analysis}).
In addition, we find that
(2)
\textbf{some interventions target the wrong deployment context}, where most model compression techniques test on high-end devices that are uncommon in the Global South (\S\ref{sec:infrastructure_analysis}).
Finally, we find that
(3)
\textbf{readiness interventions lag behind the systems they aim to govern}, where most national AI strategies predate the current LLM paradigm and have not been revised to reflect it (\S\ref{sec:readiness_analysis}).
We hope that the Global Access Framework and this survey will serve as both a diagnostic tool for identifying access barriers and a call to action for more inclusive language technology development.

The Global Access Frontier encourages a holistic framework on LLM deployment that is not solely constrained by compute.
We frame the frontier as a constrained optimization problem where not all constraints are equally restrictive.
The goal then is to identify and relax the most binding constraint to obtain the largest utility.
We introduce three key ideas:
(1) deployment contexts simultaneously face multiple constraints, such as compute, data, infrastructure, benchmarks, and/or expertise (\S\ref{sec:deployment_contexts}),
(2) the goal of deployment is to identify and relax the binding constraint (\S\ref{sec:binding_constraint}),
(3) expanding the Pareto-efficient boundary, in this framework, means making useful performance attainable across more deployment contexts.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem_formulation}

Let $\mathcal{M}$ be the space of LLM-based interventions and $\mathcal{D}$ the set of deployment contexts.
Each deployment context $d \in \mathcal{D}$ is characterized by resource availability $\res{r_d} \in \mathbb{R}^k_{\geq 0}$ across $k$ constraint dimensions, a performance function $\perf{P_d}: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}$ capturing task-aligned utility, and cost functions $\cost{c_d^{(i)}}: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ measuring resource consumption for each dimension $i$.
The best achievable performance for context $d$ is:

\vspace{-2em}
\begin{equation}
  \perf{P_d^\star} = \max_{m \in \mathcal{M}} \; \perf{P_d(m)} \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \cost{c_d^{(i)}(m)} \leq \res{r_d^{(i)}} \quad \forall i
  \label{eq:constrained-opt}
\end{equation}

In practice, we organize resource constraints into three categories that reflect barriers to LLM deployment:
access to high-quality data (\S\ref{sec:access_to_high_quality_data}), infrastructure (\S\ref{sec:access_to_infrastructure}), and operational readiness (\S\ref{sec:access_to_operational_readiness}).
We define the Global Access Frontier as the set of achievable combinations of resource and task-performance across deployment contexts, annotated by their binding constraints. %characterizing not only what performance is achievable, but \emph{why} certain contexts fall short.\footnote{The Efficiency Frontier is a special case where $k=1$ (compute only) and performance is context-independent. While this has driven progress in efficient NLP, it leaves a question unasked: \emph{efficient for whom?}}

\subsection{Binding Constraint}
\label{sec:binding_constraint}

% \input{tables/binding_constraint_diagnostic.tex}
%We define a binding constraint as a type of constraint $i$ where relaxing it would improve $\perf{P_d^\star}$, while relaxing others would not.
\citet{hausmann2008growth}'s Diagnostic Test states that a constraint $i$ is binding if it has the following properties: (D1) the price of the constraint is high,
(D2) agents, i.e., decision-makers such as researchers or developers, are trying to bypass the constraint,
(D3) less-constrained agents thrive, and
(D4) relaxing the constraint yields broad gains.
In this work, we identify three binding constraints:
access to high-quality data (\S\ref{sec:access_to_high_quality_data}),
access to infrastructure (\S\ref{sec:access_to_infrastructure}),
and access to operational readiness (\S\ref{sec:access_to_operational_readiness}).\footnote{
  In \S\ref{sec:survey_of_interventions}, we will introduce and annotate each constraint---Data, Infrastructure, Readiness---using these properties, D1--D4, to demonstrate how they are binding.
}
\citet{hausmann2005growth} operationalizes the diagnosis of binding constraints via a decision tree, which we demonstrate in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Deployment Contexts}
\label{sec:deployment_contexts}

A deployment context $d \in \mathcal{D}$ is characterized by its resource profile $r_d$ and performance requirements.
Specifically, we identify five deployment contexts: Rural, Individual, University, Industry Labs, and Enterprise Hyperscalers.
\autoref{table:deployment_contexts} shows the resource capacities for each context based on existing literature.

\paragraph{The ``Goldilocks'' Zone}
We define this zone ($\mathcal{Z}_d$) as a region in the deployment space where an intervention yields immediate utility within a community's binding resource constraints.
The size of $\mathcal{Z}_d$ is context-dependent: for communities facing multiple simultaneous bottlenecks (e.g., limited hardware and expertise), this zone is small, creating a narrow target for effective deployment.
From a \textit{supply-side} perspective, the goal is to design interventions $m$ that fit \textit{within} the existing boundaries of $\mathcal{Z}_d$ (i.e., $c_d(m) \le r_d$).
Conversely, \textit{demand-side} effects do not target the zone itself but rather aim to \textit{expand} the community's resource capacities $r_d$, enlarging $\mathcal{Z}_d$ to accommodate a wider range of interventions.

\section{Survey: NLP Interventions Based on the Constraint they Relax}
\label{sec:survey_of_interventions}

We survey \numpapers{} papers from 2021 onward, organizing interventions by the constraints they address.
For each constraint, we first discuss why it is binding, then review several interventions that relax it.


\subsection{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\paragraph{Initial Paper Screening}
To identify interventions that relax data, infrastructure, and readiness constraints, we
gather studies from peer-reviewed academic papers, industry whitepapers, and technical reports using the Semantic Scholar API.\footnote{\url{https://www.semanticscholar.org/product/api}}
Our search query includes keywords relating to multilingual NLP, efficiency, participatory AI, and LLM development (Appendix \ref{appendix:initial_paper_screening}).
The initial screen resulted in 1,018 papers.

\paragraph{Filtering and Annotation}
For each work, we perform human and LLM-assisted filtering and annotation to identify key attributes such as the work's
binding constraint,
subject domain,
downstream application,
and deployment device.
We bootstrap annotations with GPT 5 Mini\footnote{Version: \texttt{gpt-5-mini-2025-08-07}} by including the work's title and abstract in a prompt shown in \autoref{fig:appendix_annotation_prompt},
and then perform human validation to correct the annotations.

\paragraph{Final Validation}
Finally, we conduct a round of human validation to correct and refine the annotations.
In total, we collected \numpapers{} interventions that aim to relax a community's binding constraints.
Appendix \ref{appendix:extended_survey_methodology} provides details of our collection methodology.

\subsection{Access to Data}
\label{sec:access_to_high_quality_data}

Data, whether unstructured text for pretraining \citep{brown-etal-2020-language,raffel-etal-2020-exploring} or annotated labels for finetuning \citep{ouyang2022traininglanguagemodelsfollow}, is most valuable when it is high-quality and diverse.
However, collecting data for long-tail language communities is expensive and time-consuming (D1), requiring extensive human annotation \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{adelani-etal-2022-masakhaner,singh-etal-2024-aya,cahyawijaya-etal-2025-crowdsource}.
To address this scarcity (D2), researchers employ synthetic data generation \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{long-etal-2024-llms,chitale2026updeshsynthesizinggroundedinstruction} or cross-lingual transfer \citep{longpre2025atlasadaptivetransferscaling}.
Languages well-represented in pretraining corpora achieve strong downstream performance (D3) \citep{kreutzer-etal-2022-quality}, motivating language-specific development efforts (D4): Thai datasets \citep{pengpun2024seedfreesyntheticdatageneration,limkonchotiwat-etal-2025-wangchanthaiinstruct} improved SEA-LION \citep{ng2025sealionsoutheastasianlanguages}, while African-centric training \citep{oladipo-etal-2023-better} and evaluation \citep{ojo-etal-2025-afrobench} enabled AfriqueLLM \citep{yu2026afriquellmdatamixingmodel}.

\subsubsection{Training}
\label{sec:training}

Training interventions address data scarcity through three strategies:
optimizing existing resources (Data Curation),
generating new resources (Data Scaling), and
redistributing from data-rich languages (Cross-lingual Transfer).

\dataparagraph{Data Curation}
The internet serves as the primary source of pretraining data, with web crawls like Common Crawl providing petabytes of text \citep{commoncrawl}.
However, naively scraping the web yields a disproportionately English corpus as English alone comprises approximately 45\% of Common Crawl, while many languages constitute less than 0.01\% \citep{longpre-etal-2024-large}.
Extensive \textbf{Web-scale Curation} is therefore required to create balanced multilingual datasets.
Several efforts have developed pipelines for language identification, filtering, and deduplication at scale, producing multilingual corpora spanning hundreds of languages \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{xue-etal-2021-mt5,kuduguntua-etal-2023-madlad400,nguyen-etal-2024-culturax,degibert2024newmassivemultilingualdataset,oladipo-etal-2023-better,penedo2025fineweb}.
These datasets have enabled the development of more inclusive LLMs \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{martins2025eurollm9btechnicalreport,apertus2025apertusdemocratizingopencompliant,datologyai2026uberwebinsightsmultilingualcuration}.
In addition, \textbf{Data-Efficient Methods} enable strong performance from smaller corpora, easing the data burden that compounds collection costs (D1).
Some examples include quality filtering based on model perplexity \citep{marion2023moreinvestigatingdatapruning,ankner2025perplexed}, deduplication via semantic clustering \citep{tirumala-etal-2023-d4}, and establishing scaling laws for optimal data mixes \citep{ye2025datamixinglawsoptimizing}.
These techniques demonstrate that a well-curated corpus can outperform a larger, lightly-filtered one \citep{penedo2025fineweb}, lowering the data requirements for low-resource languages.
These interventions serve University, Industry, and Enterprise contexts, which have the compute to run large-scale curation pipelines;
Rural contexts benefit indirectly through the output corpora but seldom curate these themselves.

\dataparagraph{Data Scaling}
Scaling laws have shown that model performance improves predictably with the amount and quality of training data \citep{kaplan2020scalinglawsneurallanguage,chen-etal-2025-revisiting}.
However, obtaining such data for languages beyond English requires substantial human effort (D1).
Two approaches have emerged to address this challenge.
\textbf{Synthetic Data Generation} uses LLMs to produce training examples, and has been applied significantly during post-training \citep{long-etal-2024-llms}.
Large-scale synthetic datasets now exist for European \citep[Euroblocks,][]{martins2025eurollm9btechnicalreport,ramos2026eurollm22btechnicalreport}, Indic \citep[Updesh,][]{chitale2026updeshsynthesizinggroundedinstruction}, and Southeast Asian \citep[Cendol,][]{cahyawijaya-etal-2024-cendol,pengpun2024seedfreesyntheticdatageneration} languages.
Alternatively, \textbf{Translate-Train} can be used to translate existing English datasets into target languages.
This approach leverages the abundance of annotated English data, and has been shown to outperform zero-shot cross-lingual transfer for low-resource languages \citep{yu-etal-2022-translate,artetxe-etal-2023-revisiting,ebing-glavas-2024-translate}.
Both approaches target Industry Labs and Enterprise contexts that have access to capable source LLMs; however, synthetic data quality is bounded by source LLM multilingual capabilities, limiting benefits for the low-resource language communities (\autoref{fig:data_discussion}).

% and cultural personas for conditioning generation \citep{meyer2025nemotronpersonas}
%Synthetic data generation can be viewed as an \textit{off-policy} variant of \textbf{knowledge distillation} \citep{lu2025onpolicydistillation}, in contrast to \textit{on-policy} distillation \citep{agarwal2024onpolicydistillationlanguagemodels}.

\dataparagraph{Cross-lingual Transfer}
These methods leverage shared representations in multilingual models to transfer from high-resource to low-resource languages \citep{conneau-etal-2020-unsupervised,brinkmann-etal-2025-large}.
Although literature remains divided on how to best select a source language---whether by language family, custom metrics, or other criteria \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{lin-etal-2019-choosing,sun-etal-2021-cross,pham-etal-2024-unibridge}---cross-lingual transfer has proven effective in practice.
The simplest setup is \textit{training-free} \textbf{Zero-to-Few Shot Transfer}, where one applies an existing model to a downstream task (e.g., named-entity recognition, sentiment analysis).
This approach has been effective on several low-to-mid resource languages found in Africa \citep{adelani-etal-2022-masakhaner,muhammad-etal-2023-semeval}, the Philippines \citep{miranda-2023-developing,africa-etal-2025-meta}, and South America \citep{kann-etal-2022-americasnli}.
Alternatively, \textbf{Training-based Transfer} aims to learn these shared representations by finetuning on minimal data.
Prominent examples include adapter-based approaches \citep{pfeiffer-etal-2020-mad} and composable sparse fine-tuning \citep{ansell-etal-2022-composable}.
Zero-shot transfer is accessible to Individual and University contexts since it requires no additional training, while training-based methods serve University and Industry Labs contexts with compute.

\subsubsection{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

Misaligned benchmarks can make interventions appear successful while failing community needs.
We discuss what (Localized Benchmarks) and how (Evaluation Methods) to measure task-aligned performance.

\dataparagraph{Localized Benchmarks}
Standard benchmarks often fail to reflect tasks that matter to specific communities, whether defined by language or deployment context.
\textbf{Multilingual benchmarks} address this by testing a single capability---such as factual knowledge \citep{singh-etal-2025-global}, physical reasoning \citep{chang2025globalpiqaevaluatingphysical}, or alignment \citep{gureja-etal-2025-rewardbench}---across many languages.
In contrast, \textbf{Language-specific benchmarks} provide depth by evaluating multiple capabilities within a single language, often built by researchers who speak it.
This approach has proven successful for African \citep{adelani-etal-2025-irokobench,ojo-etal-2025-afrobench}, Philippine \citep{miranda-etal-2025-filbench}, Indonesian \citep{wilie-etal-2020-indonlu,cahyawijaya-etal-2021-indonlg}, Arabic \citep{qian2024camelevaladvancingculturallyaligned}, and Indic \citep{maheshwari2026indicparambenchmarkevaluatellms} languages.
Beyond language, \textbf{Community-centric benchmarks} are built with end-users for specific deployment contexts \citep{pillai-etal-2023-toward}, such as healthcare chatbots \citep{hamna2025buildingbenchmarksgroundup,ramjee-etal-2025-ashabot} or processing refugee claims \citep{barale-etal-2023-asylex}.

\dataparagraph{Evaluation Methods}
\textbf{Human-in-the-loop evaluation} remains the gold standard, particularly for subjective tasks or languages where automatic metrics are unreliable.
However, recruiting native speakers for low-resource languages is costly and difficult to scale \citep{lignos-etal-2022-toward}.
\textbf{LLM-as-a-judge} methods offer a scalable alternative, but most judges are optimized for English.
Recent work addresses this gap: \citet[M-Prometheus]{pombal2025mprometheus} provides open multilingual judges across several languages,
while \citet[mR3]{anugraha2026mr3multilingualrubricagnosticreward} extends reward modeling with reasoning capabilities.

\textbf{Synthetic data inherits its source's gaps}: although it is a widespread intervention for developing training datasets for non-English languages (\autoref{fig:data_discussion_1}), its quality is bounded by the source LLM's multilingual capabilities.
To quantify this limitation, we computed tokenizer fertility, a proxy for generation quality \citep{rust-etal-2021-good}, for several frontier LLMs across 200+ languages in FLORES-200 \citep{nllbteam2022languageleftbehindscaling}, grouping languages by resource level following \citet{joshi-etal-2020-state}.
We find that fertility degrades for lower-resource languages (\autoref{fig:data_discussion_2}), precisely those that would benefit most from synthetic augmentation.
This gap propagates into downstream design decisions: which model to distill from \citep{odumakinde2024multilingualarbitrageoptimizingdata}, which prompts to use \citep{mora2025artaskingmultilingualprompt}, and which source language to bootstrap from in cross-lingual transfer \citep{lin-etal-2019-choosing,rice-etal-2025-untangling}.
The translate-train setup also introduces translationese artifacts that can affect downstream fluency of a trained model \citep{lovenia-etal-2024-seacrowd,liu-etal-2025-translationese}.
While underexplored, some scaling laws have emerged, showing that cross-lingual transfer is predictable from shared script and language family \citep{longpre2025atlasadaptivetransferscaling}.
\textbf{Evaluation also lags behind} due to the cost of human annotation to obtain gold labels.
Although several works have used translated benchmarks, they prove to be unreliable, and LLM-as-judge methods inherit biases toward high-resource languages \citep{wu2025bitterlessonlearned2000}.
Addressing these gaps requires more principled, community-driven approaches to both data creation and evaluation.


\subsection{Access to Infrastructure}
\label{sec:access_to_infrastructure}

Infrastructure, including processing units (GPUs/TPUs), memory, storage, and energy \citep{strubell-etal-2019-energy,strubell-etal-2020-energy}, underpins LLM development but remains unevenly distributed.
The Global South faces significant gaps (D1): only 27\% of low-income populations are online \citep{itu-2024-facts}, the US and China control nearly 90\% of global GPU cluster performance \citep{epochai2025gpushare}, and training costs reach several million dollars \citep{cottier2025risingcoststrainingfrontier}.
To bypass these constraints (D2), researchers employ model compression \citep{zheng2025reviewedgeLLM} and leverage compute grants and partnerships \citep{lehdonvirta2024computenorth}.
This concentration gives the Global North substantial advantages (D3), with most LLMs originating from a few regions \citep{maslej2025artificialintelligenceindexreport}.
Yet strategic national investment can narrow these gaps (D4), as shown by the growing adoption of AI strategies in lower-income countries and regional initiatives like SEA-LION \citep{oxfordinsights2025gari,ng2025sealionsoutheastasianlanguages}.

\subsubsection{Hardware}
\label{sec:hardware}

Hardware capacity determines what models can be run.
Interventions either reduce model requirements (Smaller LLMs), expand device capabilities (On-Device ML), or build efficiency into models from the start (Efficient Architectures).

\infraparagraph{Smaller LLMs}
LLMs typically contain billions of parameters, demanding substantial compute and memory.
This class of post-hoc compression techniques aims to reduce model size during or after training.
\textbf{Knowledge Distillation} trains a smaller student model to imitate a typically larger teacher model, transferring capabilities while reducing size \citep{agarwal2024onpolicydistillationlanguagemodels,lu2025onpolicydistillation}.
The off-policy variant \citep{kim-rush-2016-sequence}, where the student learns from teacher-generated outputs, is closely related to synthetic data generation (\S\ref{sec:training}).
Another approach is \textbf{Quantization}, which reduces numerical precision of model weights and can be applied during training \citep[quantization-aware finetuning,][]{liu-etal-2024-llm} or after \citep[post-training quantization,][]{frantar2023optq,lin-etal-2025-awq}.
As \S\ref{sec:infrastructure_analysis} shows, these techniques are predominantly tested on Industry Labs and Enterprise hardware, leaving a gap for Rural and Individual contexts.

% make big models smaller

\infraparagraph{On-Device ML}
These techniques enable LLM deployment on resource-constrained devices such as mobile phones or edge devices.
\textbf{Efficient inference engines} optimize runtime execution through hardware-aware compilation and memory management, with prominent examples such as \texttt{llama.cpp} \citep{gerganov2023llamacpp}, and MLC-LLM \citep{mlc-llm}.
\textbf{Mobile Runtimes} further optimize for smartphone deployment, with frameworks like MNN-LLM achieving significant speedups over general-purpose engines \citep{wang-etal-2024-mnn}.
Finally, \textbf{Edge and TinyML} targets even more constrained environments such as microcontrollers and IoT devices, using frameworks like TensorFlow Lite Micro \citep{david2021tensorflowlitemicroembedded} and techniques to run models that exceed available RAM \citep{alizadeh-etal-2024-llm,song-etal-2024-powerinfer,tsoukas-etal-2024-review}.
These interventions directly target Rural and Individual contexts (\autoref{fig:infrastructure_discussion}); however, most mobile runtimes optimize for flagship devices, while the majority of Global South users have entry-level phones \citep{GSMA2025MobileEconomy}.

\infraparagraph{Efficient Architectures \& Adaptation}
These approaches build efficiency into model design from the start, reducing compute requirements without post-hoc compression.
\textbf{Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)} models route each token to a subset of specialized ``expert'' subnetworks, activating only a fraction of total parameters per forward pass \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{lepikhin2021gshard,fedus-etal-2022-switch,jiang2024mixtralexperts}.
\textbf{Sparse Attention} mechanisms reduce the quadratic complexity of self-attention by limiting each token's attention to a subset of positions.
Approaches include sliding window attention \citep[Longformer,][]{beltagy2020longformerlongdocumenttransformer},
and hardware-aware implementations like FlashAttention \citep{dao-etal-2022-flashattention,dao2024flashattention}.
Finally, \textbf{Parameter-Efficient Finetuning} techniques such as adapters \citep{houlsby-etal-2019-parameter} and LoRA \citep{hu2022lora,dettmers2023qlora} make finetuning accessible without large compute clusters.
MoE and sparse attention primarily benefit Industry Labs and Enterprise contexts with resources to train such architectures, while PEFT democratize finetuning for University contexts;
Rural and Individual contexts benefit only as downstream model consumers.

\subsubsection{Network}
\label{sec:network}

Network connectivity determines LLM access for deployment contexts that cannot run models locally.
Approximately 2.6B people remain offline \citep{itu-2024-facts}, and even among connected populations, bandwidth and cost constraints preclude API-based interaction.
We organize interventions into two categories: eliminating connectivity requirements entirely (Offline-First Design) or reducing connectivity costs such as bandwidth and latency (API Optimization).


\infraparagraph{Offline-First Design}
For deployment contexts with intermittent or no connectivity, the only viable path is running the inference pipeline locally.
\textbf{Portable model formats} make this possible, particularly the GGUF format that packages model parameters into a single binary file \citep{gerganov2023llamacpp,turc2025gguf}.
These formats are typically paired with local inference servers such as Ollama, \texttt{llama.cpp}, and LMStudio.
\citet{park2025surveyinferenceengineslarge} note that Ollama and \texttt{llama.cpp} rank highest for ease of deployment among edge devices.
Alternatively, \textbf{Browser-based inference} eliminates local software installation by running LLM inference entirely within browsers via WebGPU \citep{ruan2024webllmhighperformanceinbrowserllm}.
Together, these approaches enable LLM access without relying on external infrastructure.
These serve Rural and Individual contexts where connectivity is the binding constraint (\autoref{fig:infrastructure_discussion}), but still requiring devices capable of local inference.

\infraparagraph{API Optimization}
Interacting with Cloud APIs is the primary mode of access in most frontier LLMs.
However, API access introduces three barriers: round-trip latency, bandwidth consumption, and per-token pricing.
\textbf{Semantic caching} attempts to reduce the cost of round-trip latency by storing and reusing responses for semantically similar queries \citep{bang-2023-gptcache,regmi2024gptsemanticcachereducing}.
\textbf{Hybrid inference} adapts techniques such as speculative decoding \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{li-etal-2025-sled,ning2025dssd,venkatesha2025fast} for bandwidth-constrained deployment contexts, which reduces communication payloads across API calls.
Finally, \textbf{Prompt compression} reduces the number of tokens transmitted per request, directly lowering both bandwidth consumption and API costs.
The most prominent work on prompt compression is the LLMLingua series that uses a small LLM to identify and remove low-information tokens, achieving up to 20x compression with minimal performance degradation \citep{jiang-etal-2023-llmlingua,jiang-etal-2024-longllmlingua,pan-etal-2024-llmlingua}.
These optimizations benefit Individual and University contexts that have connectivity but face bandwidth or cost constraints; however, they assume baseline internet access, leaving Rural contexts with intermittent connectivity unserved.

\subsubsection{Privacy}
\label{sec:privacy}

Infrastructure constraints due to privacy emerge when deployment contexts require models but cannot share data.
These constraints are common in domains such as healthcare, legal services, and finance, where sensitive data cannot leave local infrastructure due to regulatory restrictions like HIPAA or the inherent sensitivity of the information itself.
We discuss techniques for training without exposing data (Privacy-preserving Training), and methods for performing inference on sensitive data (Privacy-preserving Inference).

\infraparagraph{Privacy-preserving Training}
This class of methods enables model training while keeping raw data private.
\textbf{Federated Learning} (FL) is an approach that allows multiple clients to collaboratively train a shared model by exchanging only model updates rather than raw data \citep{mcmahan-etal-2017-communication,sun-etal-2023-decentralized}.
This setup has been extended to LLM finetuning \citep{kuang-etal-2024-federatedscopellm}, and has been applied to multilingual contexts where culturally-sensitive data remain hidden \citep{weller-etal-2022-pretrained}.
Several frameworks enable FL, such as \texttt{flower} \citep{beutel2022flowerfriendlyfederatedlearning} and FATE-LLM \citep{liu-etal-2021-fate}.
\textbf{Differential Privacy} is a conceptual framework that guarantees an algorithm will not reveal details about an individual's data.
Under this framework, several works explored generating differentially private synthetic data \citep{amin-etal-2024-private}, private finetuning \citep{chua2024mind}, and data minimization strategies \citep{zhou2025operationalizingdataminimizationprivacypreserving}.
These methods primarily serve Enterprise and Industry Labs contexts operating under regulatory constraints (e.g., healthcare, finance); Rural and Individual contexts rarely face formal privacy compliance requirements, though they may have cultural sensitivities around data sharing that these techniques could address.

\infraparagraph{Privacy-preserving Inference}
This class of methods enables users to query LLMs without exposing sensitive inputs to the model provider.
\textbf{Cryptographic Methods} allow computation on encrypted data, ensuring that raw inputs remain hidden throughout inference.
Fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) enables arithmetic operations directly on ciphertexts, with works such as EncryptedLLM \citep{castro2025encryptedllm} and THOR \citep{moon-etal-2025-thor} applying FHE to transformer inference.
Secure multi-party computation (SMPC) distributes computation across multiple parties using secret sharing, so that no single party observes the complete input \citep{li2024surveyprivatetransformerinference}.
However, these methods introduce substantial computational overhead, limiting their practicality for latency-sensitive applications.
Alternatively, \textbf{Input Sanitization} methods protect privacy by removing or obfuscating sensitive spans before sending queries to a remote server.
PrivacyRestore \citep{zeng-etal-2025-privacyrestore} removes privacy-sensitive spans from user inputs and restores them via activation steering during generation, preserving output quality while avoiding exposure of raw data.
Cryptographic methods target Enterprise contexts with strict compliance needs but introduce prohibitive computational overhead for resource-constrained settings.

This gap compounds with other constraints: compression techniques like quantization disproportionately degrade performance on non-Latin scripts \citep{marchisio-etal-2024-quantization}, and knowledge distillation requires teacher-student tokenizer alignment, potentially excluding long-tail languages entirely \citep{gholami-etal-2024-gold,minixhofer2025universal}.
Rural contexts remain underserved: their hardware constraints bind hardest, yet the interventions designed to relax them are rarely validated on representative devices.


\subsection{Access to Operational Readiness}
\label{sec:access_to_operational_readiness}

Data (\S\ref{sec:access_to_high_quality_data}) and Infrastructure (\S\ref{sec:access_to_infrastructure}) are supply-side: agents design interventions that fit within $\mathcal{Z}_d$.
Operational readiness is demand-side: it expands the community's resource capacities to accommodate more interventions.
We define readiness as the operational capacity of a deployment context to adopt and sustain LLM-based interventions, encompassing technical expertise and institutional support.\footnote{
  This framing draws on \citet{cohen-levinthal-1990-absorptive}'s ``absorptive capacity'' framework
  and \citet{sen-2001-development}'s Capabilities Framework, both of which posit that
  development involves expanding a community's capabilities.
}
However, NLP expertise is scarce and expensive globally (D1), more so in the Global South:
less than 5\% of the population in low-income countries has basic digital skills, compared to 66\% in the Global North \citep{worldbank-2025-digital-progress}.
\citet{oxfordinsights2025gari} shows a strong correlation between a country's readiness scores and actual adoption (D3).
To address this constraint (D2), grassroots and community-led initiatives have emerged to provide tools, datasets, and technical expertise.
Investing in these efforts has proven valuable (D4):
grassroots communities in Africa, Southeast Asia, and Latin America have produced resources that benefit their communities and trained researchers for their language.
% Masakhane has produced several datasets and models to support African languages,
% and similar patterns have been observed in Southeast Asia with SEACrowd.


\subsubsection{Expertise}
\label{sec:expertise}

Expertise, whether from NLP researchers in academia or practitioners in industry, is essential for developing and deploying LLMs.
We describe how such expertise can be cultivated from the ground up (Community-led Development) or from the top down (Capacity Building) in terms of deployment context.

\readinessparagraph{Community-led Development}
In the Individual deployment context, \textbf{Grassroots Communities} have emerged as a common approach to developing expertise.
Masakhane is a distributed volunteer network of over 2,000 researchers working on African NLP, producing datasets, benchmarks, and models for the region \citep[\textit{inter alia}]{nekoto-etal-2020-participatory,dossou-etal-2022-afrolm,adelani-etal-2022-masakhaner,adelani-etal-2023-masakhanews,adelani-etal-2025-irokobench}.
Similarly, SEACrowd is a volunteer-driven initiative focused on Southeast Asian NLP \citep{lovenia-etal-2024-seacrowd,cahyawijaya-etal-2025-crowdsource}.
Beyond resource creation, these communities also offer mentorship programs that have led to conference publications \citep{cahyawijaya2025seacrowdapprentice}.
In the University deployment context, \textbf{Special Interest Groups} organize researchers within formal academic settings.
Examples include AI4Bharat for Indic NLP, AmericasNLP for Indigenous languages of the Americas,
and ACL SIGs like SIGTYP and SIGARAB.

\readinessparagraph{Capacity Building}
In the Enterprise Hyperscaler deployment context, companies have released \textbf{Open Multilingual Models} that benefit low-resource language communities.
Examples include Meta's NLLB-200 for machine translation across 200 languages \citep{nllbteam2022languageleftbehindscaling}, Google's TranslateGemma supporting translation across 55 languages \citep{finkelstein2026translategemmatechnicalreport}, and Cohere for AI's Aya initiative, which engaged 3,000 contributors across 119 countries to build models for 101 languages \citep{singh-etal-2024-aya}.
Beyond deployment contexts, \textbf{Grants and Foundations} provide funding for dataset creation and NLP research.
The Lacuna Fund has awarded grants totaling \$9M USD to support NLP datasets for low-resource languages \citep{lacunafund2023report}, while Translated's Imminent program funds research on language technologies.
At the national level, \textbf{AI Strategies} increasingly prioritize local language support \citep{oxfordinsights2025gari,lu2026buyversusbuildllm}.
The African Union's Continental AI Strategy \citep{africanunion2024continental} emphasizes localized datasets reflecting African languages and values, with several countries incorporating multilinguality into their AI roadmaps.

\subsubsection{Analysis}
\label{sec:readiness_analysis}

National AI strategies are the primary policy instrument for building operational readiness,
yet many are \textbf{temporally misaligned with the pace of LLM development.}
To quantify this gap, we mapped the publication dates of national AI strategies against major LLM releases (\autoref{fig:readiness_discussion}).
We find that many countries in the Global South have not published a strategy at all.
High-income countries mitigate this through revision cycles, while lower-income countries often operate under outdated strategies.
Rural, Individual, and University contexts in the Global South are most affected: they lack the institutional capacity to adapt to shifting paradigms, and by the time readiness catches up, the frontier has already moved.

\section{Discussion: Identifying the Binding Constraint}
\label{sec:discussion}

To put the framework into practice, we apply the diagnostic test (\S\ref{sec:binding_constraint}) to an LLM-based application for supporting clinical health workers (CHWs) from \citet{rutunda2026llmfrontline}'s study.
We walk through the decision tree, repeatedly asking \textit{why} a constraint binds until we reach a root cause (\autoref{fig:binding_constraint_decision_tree}).
The tree first tests infrastructure: CHWs have devices and network access, ruling out hardware.
However, deployment costs are high not because per-token API prices are unreasonable, but because Kinyarwanda's subword tokenization produces more tokens per query than English \citep{ahia2023do}, inflating costs at scale.
The root cause is \textbf{data}: poor tokenizer coverage of Kinyarwanda, a constraint that no amount of infrastructure investment would resolve.
This illustrates how the framework steers practitioners toward the right lever, i.e., improving tokenizer representation rather than subsidizing compute.

\section{Conclusion}
We introduce the Global Access Frontier, framing LLM deployment as a constrained optimization problem across data, infrastructure, and readiness, and survey works from 2021 onward that attempt to relax these constraints.
We also demonstrate how this framework can be used to identify a community's binding constraint via a case study.
We hope that the Global Access Framework will help researchers and practitioners develop inclusive language technologies.

\subsection{Filtering and Annotation}

\paragraph{Relevance Filtering}
In order to ensure that the selected papers for study are relevant, we perform a combination of rules-based and human relevance filtering.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=5mm,topsep=0mm,itemsep=0mm]
  \item \textbf{Rule-based Filtering:} We retain only papers with $\texttt{minCitations} \geq 5$ to ensure sufficient scholarly impact. This brought down the number of papers to around 600$+$.
  \item \textbf{Human filtering:} In this step, we scan the title and the abstract and include the paper if and only if it fulfills the following criteria:
        \begin{enumerate}
          \item \textit{Does it target edge deployment?} Does the paper explicitly discuss deployment on resource-constrained settings?
          \item \textit{Is it focused on NLP?} Does the paper's primary contribution involve natural language processing tasks?
          \item \textit{Does it present a technical contribution?} Does the paper present a concrete technical approach such as a model architecture, training method, or system optimization?
        \end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{LLM and Human Annotation}
In order to obtain richer metadata, we annotate each work across several dimensions.
We bootstrap annotations using an LLM and then perform human verification.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=5mm,topsep=0mm,itemsep=0mm]
  \item \textbf{Subject Areas:} We use ARR's area keywords,\footnote{\url{https://web.archive.org/web/20251204163901/https://aclrollingreview.org/areas}} which include subject areas such as
        Efficient/Low-Resource Methods for NLP, Generation, Information Extraction, and Machine Translation, among others.
  \item \textbf{Application Domain:} We combine the taxonomies presented by \citet{roges-etal-2023-qadatasetexplosion} and \citet{chen2024a}, which describe common end-user applications of NLP.
        Our application domains include: Healthcare, Legal, Finance, Agriculture, Smart City, and Manufacturing.
  \item \textbf{Interventions:} We follow previous surveys by \citet{treviso-etal-2023-efficient} and \citet{zheng2025reviewedgeLLM}, which outline techniques in efficient NLP and edge deployment. See \S\ref{sec:survey_of_interventions} for the full list.
  \item \textbf{Deployment Device:} We categorize each work by where an edge model can be deployed. Following \citet{zheng2025reviewedgeLLM}, this includes Cloud, Mobile, and IoT/Microcontrollers, among others.
  \item \textbf{Supported Languages:} For each work, we note whether the model supports languages beyond English. We determine this by examining the evaluation setup: if the work evaluated on multilingual benchmarks, we record which languages were included.
\end{itemize}
